## üìä Citing Derived Work

### If You Test Predictions

If you conduct experiments testing our predictions:

**Your paper should cite:**
1. This repository (for the original framework and predictions)
2. Your own work (for the empirical findings)

**Example:**
```
We tested the substrate-task matching hypothesis proposed by Danan (2025) 
and found [your results]. Our methodology extended the original protocol 
by [your contributions].

References:
Danan, H. (2025). Computational substrates... [full citation]
[Your paper citation]
```

### If You Extend the Framework

If you apply the framework to new substrates or domains:

**Example:**
```
Building on the substrate-aware framework (Danan, 2025), we analyze 
quantum computing substrates and derive novel predictions...

References:
Danan, H. (2025). Computational substrates... [full citation]
[Your paper citation]
```

## üôè Why Citation Matters

**For science:**
- Tracks influence and impact of ideas
- Enables others to find original source
- Gives credit for intellectual contributions

**For this project:**
- Demonstrates value to funding agencies
- Helps justify time investment
- Encourages continued development
- Shows which ideas are most useful

**For you:**
- Strengthens your work by citing sources
- Follows academic integrity standards
- Helps readers find related work

---

## üí° Citation Don'ts

**Don't:**
- Cite without reading (cite-by-proxy)
- Misrepresent findings or claims
- Cite only to criticize without engaging substance
- Forget to cite if you use the ideas

**Do:**
- Cite fairly and accurately
- Engage with the substance of arguments
- Give credit generously
- Help others find good work
